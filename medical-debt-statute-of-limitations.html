Medical debt · legal time limits

Medical debt statute of limitations by state (2026) — all 50 states

Every state sets a legal deadline — the statute of limitations — on how long a hospital or debt collector can sue you for an unpaid medical bill. Once that deadline passes, the debt is time-barred: they can still call you, but they cannot win in court. This guide covers all 50 states, what resets the clock, the dangerous "zombie debt" problem, and exactly what to say if you're sued on old medical debt.

What it means

What the statute of limitations on medical debt actually means

The statute of limitations (SOL) is a state law that sets a deadline for creditors to file a lawsuit to collect a debt. For medical debt, the clock typically starts on the date of your last payment or the date the debt first became delinquent — whichever is later.

Once the SOL expires, the debt is "time-barred." This means:

  • They cannot win a lawsuit against you

    If you raise the expired statute of limitations as a defense in court, the case must be dismissed. However — and this is critical — the court does not automatically raise this defense for you. You must assert it in your written response to the lawsuit.

    Your defense

  • They can still contact you

    An expired SOL does not prevent debt collectors from calling, writing, or asking for payment. It only removes their ability to sue successfully. Collection calls on time-barred debt are legal in most states — annoying, but legal.

    Still allowed

  • The debt does not legally disappear

    You still technically owe the money. The SOL is a procedural defense in court, not a legal forgiveness of the debt. Voluntarily paying a time-barred debt is your choice — but making any payment may restart the clock.

    Important

  • Credit reporting is a separate timeline

    The SOL and the 7-year credit reporting window under the FCRA are entirely separate. A debt can be time-barred (uncollectable in court) but still appearing on your credit report — or vice versa.

    Separate rules

What resets the clock

Actions that restart the statute of limitations — the zombie debt danger

This is the most dangerous and least understood aspect of the statute of limitations. Several actions can restart the SOL clock, giving the creditor a fresh window to sue even on very old debt. Before taking any action on an old medical bill, understand these risks:

⚠️

Making any payment

Even a $5 payment on a time-barred debt restarts the SOL clock in most states. This is the most common trap. Never make a "good faith" payment on an old bill before researching whether it's time-barred.

⚠️

Signing a payment agreement

Agreeing in writing to a payment plan — even without making a payment — can restart the clock in many states. Get legal advice before signing anything regarding old medical debt.

⚠️

Written acknowledgment of the debt

Writing a letter or email that acknowledges you owe the debt may restart the clock in some states. Be careful about what you put in writing when communicating with collectors about old bills.

✅

Disputing the debt in writing

Sending a written dispute letter under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act does NOT restart the clock. Disputing is safe — it's a legal right that protects you. You have 30 days from first collector contact to dispute in writing.

Zombie debt

What is zombie debt — and how collectors use it against you

"Zombie debt" is old, time-barred debt that collectors try to revive — either by getting you to make a payment (restarting the SOL clock), or by suing you hoping you won't raise the expired SOL as a defense.

Zombie debt is a serious and documented problem in medical debt collection. Debt buyers purchase portfolios of old medical debt for pennies on the dollar, then aggressively pursue collection — including filing lawsuits — on debts that may be years past the statute of limitations. They count on the fact that most defendants don't show up to court or don't know to raise the SOL defense.

How to protect yourself from zombie debt tactics:

  • Never ignore a lawsuit summons — always respond in writing, even if the debt seems old
  • In your response, include the defense that the debt is time-barred if you believe it may be
  • Never make a payment on old debt without first researching whether it's time-barred
  • Request debt validation in writing within 30 days of first collector contact
  • Report collectors who sue on time-barred debt they know is expired to the CFPB and your state attorney general — this may violate the FDCPA
If you're sued

Exactly what to do — and say — if you're sued on old medical debt

  1. Count the days from last activity

    Calculate the number of years since your last payment or the date the debt first went delinquent. Compare this to your state's SOL in the table above. If it's past the limit, the SOL is your primary defense.

  2. File a written answer before the deadline

    You typically have 20–30 days from being served to file a written answer. File it even if the debt is obviously time-barred — courts do not automatically dismiss time-barred cases without your response. Never ignore a summons.

  3. Include the statute of limitations as an affirmative defense

    In your written answer, include language like: "Defendant asserts that Plaintiff's claims are barred by the applicable statute of limitations under [your state statute citation]." This is called an affirmative defense — you must raise it, or it is waived.

  4. Gather evidence of the debt's age

    Collect documentation showing when the debt originated and when you last made a payment — statements, old bills, bank records. This evidence supports your SOL defense if the creditor disputes the timeline.

  5. Contact free legal aid if the amount is significant

    Many legal aid organizations have experience with time-barred debt lawsuits. For a significant bill, getting free legal representation dramatically increases your chances of successfully asserting the SOL defense and having the case dismissed.

Credit report vs. statute of limitations

The two timelines — credit reporting and statute of limitations — are completely separate

This is one of the most common points of confusion about medical debt. The statute of limitations (how long they can sue you) and the credit reporting period (how long it stays on your credit report) operate on entirely different clocks, governed by different laws.
