Medical debt · wage garnishment

Can a hospital garnish your wages for a medical bill?

Yes — in most US states, a hospital or debt collector can legally garnish your wages to collect unpaid medical debt. But there is a critical catch: they cannot do it simply by sending you a bill or a collection notice. They must sue you, win a court judgment, and then obtain a separate garnishment order — a process that takes months and gives you multiple opportunities to stop it. Several states ban it entirely. Here is everything you need to know in 2026.

If you have an unpaid hospital bill and have not yet been sued, you still have time to prevent garnishment entirely. How it works

The exact legal process a hospital must follow before garnishing your wages

This is the most misunderstood aspect of medical wage garnishment. A hospital billing department cannot call your employer and start taking your paycheck. There is a multi-step legal process that must happen first — and every step is an opportunity for you to intervene.

  1. Step 1 — The hospital or collector files a civil lawsuit

    After collection efforts fail, the hospital (or a debt buyer who purchased your account) files a civil lawsuit against you in your local court. For smaller debts this is often small claims court; for larger amounts it goes to general civil court. You are served with a summons and complaint — typically by a process server or certified mail. This is when the clock starts on your response window, usually 20–30 days depending on your state.

  2. Step 2 — You must respond or a default judgment is entered

    If you do nothing, the court automatically enters a default judgment in the creditor's favor. This is the single most common mistake people make — and it eliminates all your defenses. You must file a written answer, even a simple one, to preserve your rights. Many court systems have free self-help centers that can assist you in drafting a basic response.

  3. Step 3 — The court enters a judgment

    Either through a default (you didn't respond), a settlement, or a court hearing, a judgment is eventually entered for some amount. The judgment is a court order saying you legally owe the money. At this point the creditor has significantly more power — but they still cannot automatically garnish wages.

  4. Step 4 — The creditor applies for a garnishment order

    With a judgment in hand, the creditor applies to the court for a garnishment order — a separate legal document directing your employer to withhold a portion of your pay. In some states you receive notice of this application and have the right to object. In others it happens with little warning. Your employer receives the order and is legally required to comply.

  5. Step 5 — Your employer withholds and sends payments to the creditor

    Once the garnishment order is active, your employer withholds the specified amount from each paycheck and sends it directly to the creditor — bypassing you entirely. The garnishment continues until the debt is paid, the creditor agrees to stop, or a court orders it stopped.

The 240-day window changes everything

At nonprofit hospitals, federal law (IRS 501(r)) prohibits any "Extraordinary Collection Action" — including filing a lawsuit — for at least 240 days from your first bill. That is eight months during which the hospital legally cannot initiate the lawsuit process that leads to garnishment. Apply for charity care now if you are within this window.

States that ban it

As of July 2026, several states ban wage garnishment for medical debt entirely or have recently passed significant new protections. This is a rapidly changing area of law — 8 states introduced new legislation in early 2026 alone.

If you live in one of the states above with a complete ban, a hospital or debt collector threatening wage garnishment for a medical bill is either mistaken or attempting to deceive you. You can safely inform them that your state law prohibits wage garnishment for private creditor debts — and if they persist, report them to your state attorney general's consumer protection office.

How much can be taken

Federal and state limits on how much of your paycheck can be garnished

Even in states where medical wage garnishment is allowed, federal law sets strict limits on how much can be taken from each paycheck. These limits are set by the Consumer Credit Protection Act (CCPA) and apply in every state.

Your weekly disposable earningsMaximum garnishment (federal limit)
$217.50 or less (30 × federal minimum wage)$0 — completely exempt
$217.51 to $290Amount over $217.50 only
Over $29025% of disposable earnings

"Disposable earnings" means your pay after legally required deductions — taxes, Social Security, Medicare. It does not include voluntary deductions like health insurance premiums or 401(k) contributions. Your employer calculates this for each pay period.

Many states set limits that are more protective than the federal floor. For example, California limits garnishment to 25% of disposable earnings or the amount above 40 times the state minimum wage — whichever is less. Since California's minimum wage is higher than federal, this protects more of your paycheck in practice. Always check your specific state's garnishment limits in addition to the federal rule.

Protected income types

Income that cannot be garnished for medical debt

  • Social Security benefits (SSI and SSDI)

    Social Security income is federally protected from garnishment by private creditors including hospitals and medical debt collectors. This applies to both SSI (Supplemental Security Income) and SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance). If your only income is Social Security, your wages cannot be garnished for medical debt under any circumstances.

    Fully protected

  • VA disability benefits

    Veterans Affairs disability compensation is federally protected from garnishment by private creditors. Medical debt collectors cannot garnish VA disability payments.

    Fully protected

  • Federal retirement benefits

    Federal employee retirement benefits (FERS, CSRS) are protected from private creditor garnishment. State pension benefits also often have strong protections — check your specific state.

    Protected

  • Unemployment benefits

    Unemployment compensation is generally protected from private creditor garnishment in most states, though rules vary. Check your state's specific exemption rules.

    Generally protected

  • Workers' compensation

    Workers' compensation benefits are generally exempt from private creditor garnishment. If your only income is workers' comp, a garnishment order should not be able to reach it.

    Generally protected

Bank account protections for Social Security

Even if your bank account contains Social Security funds, those deposits are protected from levy. Federal rules require banks to automatically protect two months of Social Security deposits in your account when they receive a levy order. However, if your account contains mixed funds (both Social Security and other income), the calculation becomes more complex — contact a legal aid attorney if you receive a bank levy involving Social Security funds.

How to stop it

How to stop wage garnishment for a hospital bill — at every stage

Before lawsuit

Apply for charity care

The single most powerful action. Applying for charity care at the original hospital pauses all collection action under federal 501(r) rules. If approved, the debt may be reduced to zero — eliminating garnishment risk entirely. Use our eligibility calculator to check your chances.

Before lawsuit

Negotiate a payment plan

Contact the hospital billing department or collection agency and propose a monthly payment plan you can actually maintain. Most will agree — garnishment is expensive for them too. Get any agreement in writing before making the first payment.

After lawsuit filed

Respond — don't ignore the summons

Filing a written answer to the lawsuit preserves all your defenses and forces the creditor to prove their case. A default judgment from ignoring the summons eliminates your options. Many court self-help centers will assist you for free.

After lawsuit filed

Check the statute of limitations

If the debt is older than your state's statute of limitations for medical debt (typically 3–6 years), you may have a complete defense. Raise this in your written answer. See our statute of limitations guide for every state.

After judgment

Negotiate a settlement

Even after a judgment, most creditors will accept a lump-sum settlement for less than the full amount — often 40–60 cents on the dollar. A settled judgment stops garnishment. Get the settlement agreement in writing and get a court-filed satisfaction of judgment.

Active garnishment

Claim an exemption

If your income is below your state's garnishment exemption threshold, or if your income is from a protected source like Social Security, file an exemption claim with the court. Your state's court self-help center can help you file the right form.

Frequently asked questions

Medical wage garnishment: common questions answered

Can a hospital garnish wages without a lawsuit?

No — never. A hospital or debt collector must file a lawsuit, win a court judgment, and then obtain a separate garnishment order before any wages can be withheld. Anyone claiming they can garnish your wages without a court order is either wrong or attempting to deceive you.

How long does the garnishment process take from unpaid bill to garnishment?

At nonprofit hospitals, the earliest a lawsuit can begin is after 240 days from your first bill. Add time for the lawsuit itself (typically 2–6 months for an uncontested case), plus time to obtain the garnishment order. Realistically, 12–24 months from unpaid bill to active garnishment is typical for those who do nothing — giving significant time to intervene.

Can my employer fire me because of wage garnishment?

Federal law protects employees from termination for a single wage garnishment order. However, this protection does not apply if you have more than one garnishment order simultaneously. Some states provide stronger protections — check your state's employment law for specifics.

Does charity care stop garnishment that's already started?

Potentially yes. If the original nonprofit hospital approves a charity care application even after the debt has gone to collections or a judgment has been entered, the hospital may recall the debt from the collector — which would stop the garnishment. It is always worth contacting the original hospital and asking about a retroactive charity care application.

Can bankruptcy stop wage garnishment for medical debt?

Yes. Filing for bankruptcy triggers an "automatic stay" that immediately halts all collection actions including active wage garnishments. Medical debt is generally fully dischargeable in Chapter 7 bankruptcy. Bankruptcy is a legitimate legal tool — but it has lasting credit consequences. Consult a bankruptcy attorney before deciding.

What if I can't afford the garnishment amount?

Contact the creditor and request a voluntary payment plan in lieu of garnishment — most prefer consistent payments over the administrative cost of maintaining a garnishment. You can also file a motion with the court to modify the garnishment amount if it causes undue hardship. A legal aid attorney can help you file this motion for free if your income is low.
