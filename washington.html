State guide

Hospital charity care in Washington State

Washington State law sets a mandatory charity care floor for nonprofit hospitals — but many of the state's largest hospital systems go significantly beyond the legal minimum, making Washington one of the more patient-friendly states for financial assistance seekers.

Income thresholds Key protections Find your policy
Law

RCW 70.170

Washington's Hospital Licensing Act (RCW 70.170) requires all licensed hospitals to provide charity care and establishes minimum income thresholds for free and reduced-cost care.

Free care

Under 100% FPL

State law requires free care for patients at or below 100% of the federal poverty level — all licensed hospitals must comply.

Discounts

Up to 200% FPL

Sliding-scale discounts are required for patients between 100% and 200% FPL, with specific tiers set by each hospital's policy.

Beyond the law

Many hospitals extend further

Providence, UW Medicine, MultiCare, and other large systems voluntarily extend meaningful discounts well above 200% FPL — always check the specific hospital's published policy.

Income thresholds

Washington's charity care income thresholds

Washington law sets a minimum floor, but hospital practice often exceeds it. Here's how the tiers typically work:

Income levelRequired or typical benefit
Under 100% FPL ($15,960 for 1 person)100% free care — required by state law
100%–200% FPLSliding-scale discount — required by state law
200%–400% FPLDiscount available at many hospitals (voluntary)
Above 400% FPLDepends on individual hospital policy

For 2026, 200% FPL for a family of four is $66,000 per year. Providence Health, one of Washington's largest systems, extends charity care to patients up to 400% FPL — check the specific hospital you were treated at for exact tiers.

Key protections

Key protections under Washington charity care law

  • Mandatory charity care program

    Every licensed hospital in Washington — including for-profit facilities — must maintain a charity care program. This is broader than the federal 501(r) rule.

    Required

  • Uniform application form

    Washington state provides a standard charity care application form that all hospitals must accept, making the process more consistent across facilities.

    Standardized

  • No collections during review

    Hospitals must pause collection efforts while a charity care application is under review.

    Protected

  • Annual reporting

    Hospitals must report their charity care data annually to the Washington State Department of Health, creating public accountability.

    Transparent

Washington Apple Health (Medicaid) first

Before applying for hospital charity care, check if you qualify for Washington Apple Health (Medicaid). Washington expanded Medicaid under the ACA, so many low-income residents qualify for full coverage. If you're under 138% FPL, Apple Health may cover your bills more completely than charity care.

Find your hospital's policy

How to look up a specific hospital's rules

The Washington State Department of Health maintains hospital licensing data and charity care reporting. To find your hospital's specific policy, visit the hospital's website and search for "financial assistance" or "charity care" — all hospitals must post this publicly. You can also call the hospital's patient financial services department directly and ask for the charity care application. Washington's standardized application form must be accepted by all hospitals.
